DCF Basics

Understand what discounted cash flow means, why investors use it, and how each ingredient fits together.

What is Discounted Cash Flow?

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) is a valuation method that estimates the intrinsic value of a business or project by projecting the cash it will generate in the future and converting those amounts into today's dollars using a discount rate.

Instead of asking, “What is the stock price right now?” DCF asks, “How much cash could this investment realistically produce over time, and what is that worth in today's money after accounting for risk and the time value of money?”

Four Key Ingredients

Cash Flow

The money left over after a business pays its operating expenses, taxes, and necessary investments in equipment or software. This is the cash that can be returned to investors.

Growth

Reasonable assumptions for how cash flows might increase (or decrease) over time based on the business model, industry, and competitive landscape.

Discount Rate

A percentage that reflects risk and opportunity cost. Higher risk or higher return expectations mean using a higher discount rate when valuing future cash flows.

Terminal Value

An estimate of what the business could be worth after the forecast period ends, based on a steady long-term growth assumption.

When Should You Use DCF?

DCF is especially helpful when you care about the long-term earning power of a company rather than short-term price movements. It works best when:

  • You can make reasonable forecasts for revenue, margins, and reinvestment for at least a few years.
  • You want to compare the value implied by cash flows to the current market price.
  • You are comfortable thinking in terms of scenarios instead of a single “perfect” prediction.

In later pages we will walk through a full example and a simple sensitivity analysis so you can see how changing growth or discount rate assumptions affects intrinsic value.
