Step-by-Step DCF Example

Follow a simple five-step example to move from forecasts to an estimate of intrinsic value.

Watch a Simple Perpetuity DCF

This short video shows a quick DCF-style valuation for a mature company with stable cash flows. It treats free cash flow like a perpetuity and uses a single discount rate to jump straight from cash flow to enterprise value, equity value, and an estimated share price.

Think of this as an intuition-building shortcut. The five-step example below walks through a more detailed, multi-year forecast and sensitivity analysis.

Step 1: Forecast Free Cash Flows

Imagine a company that generated $50 million in free cash flow last year. You research its business and decide it is reasonable to assume cash flows can grow 5% per year for the next five years.

You build a simple forecast table in a spreadsheet that projects cash flow for Year 1 through Year 5 by applying this growth rate each year.

Year Growth assumption Free cash flow (USD, millions)
Base year (just reported) — $50.0
Year 1 +5% $52.5
Year 2 +5% $55.1
Year 3 +5% $57.9
Year 4 +5% $60.8
Year 5 +5% $63.8

These figures are rounded to one decimal place to keep the example simple. In a real model you would likely keep more precision.

Step 2: Choose a Discount Rate

Next, you need a discount rate that reflects the risk of the cash flows and your required return. For a stable, profitable company, you might use 8–10%. For a riskier, early-stage business, you might use 12% or more.

In this example we will use 10% as our base discount rate and later test how the valuation changes at 8% and 12%.

Step 3: Estimate a Terminal Value

After Year 5, you assume the company's cash flows grow at a modest long-term rate (for example 2–3%) rather than the higher short-term growth used in the explicit forecast period.

You can estimate the terminal value using a formula that divides the Year 6 cash flow by the difference between the discount rate and the long-term growth rate. That terminal value is then treated as a large cash flow in Year 5.

In our example, if you assume a 3% long-term growth rate after Year 5, Year 6 cash flow would be approximately $65.7 million ($63.8 million × 1.03). The terminal value formula would be:

Terminal Value = Year 6 cash flow ÷ (discount rate − long-term growth) = $65.7 million ÷ (10% − 3%).

Step 4: Discount Everything Back to Today

Now you discount each of the five yearly cash flows and the terminal value back to today using the 10% discount rate. This converts future dollars into present values that are directly comparable.

The sum of all discounted cash flows represents your estimate of the value of the operating business. You can then adjust for cash, debt, and the number of shares to arrive at an intrinsic value per share.

Year Cash flow (USD, millions) Present value formula at 10%
Year 1 $52.5 52.5 ÷ (1.10)1
Year 2 $55.1 55.1 ÷ (1.10)2
Year 3 $57.9 57.9 ÷ (1.10)3
Year 4 $60.8 60.8 ÷ (1.10)4
Year 5 $63.8 63.8 ÷ (1.10)5
Terminal value TV from Step 3 TV ÷ (1.10)5

When you actually run these numbers in a spreadsheet and add up all of the present values, you arrive at an overall estimate of what the business is worth today based on your assumptions.

Step 5: Run a Simple Sensitivity Check

Because nobody can predict the future perfectly, it is helpful to see how your valuation changes if growth or the discount rate are a little higher or lower than your base case.

Discount ↓ / Growth → 3% growth 5% growth 7% growth
8% discount rate $90 per share $98 per share $107 per share
10% discount rate $78 per share $85 per share $93 per share
12% discount rate $69 per share $75 per share $82 per share

These numbers are illustrative, but they show how a higher discount rate or lower growth assumption can quickly lower your estimate of intrinsic value.

5-Step Checklist

  • Understand the business model first.
  • Forecast free cash flow for 3–5 years.
  • Pick a reasonable discount rate range.
  • Estimate a conservative terminal value.
  • Test optimistic and pessimistic scenarios.

Next Up

After you are comfortable with this example, try building your own DCF in a spreadsheet using a company you know well, then compare your intrinsic value estimate to its current market price.
